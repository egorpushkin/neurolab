What is software piracy? Why should I be concerned about it?
The term software piracy covers different activities, including: illegal copying of programs, counterfeiting and the distribution of infringing software, and sometimes even sharing a program with a friend. The Business Software Alliance's 2002 Global Piracy Study reports that two out of every five business software applications in use, worldwide, are unlicensed or stolen. In some countries or regions, as many as nine out of every ten business software applications in use are unlicensed or stolen.
When a consumer decides to use an unauthorized copy of a software program, the consumer forfeits his or her right to the support, documentation, warranties and periodic updates provided by the software manufacturer. In addition, pirated software can contain viruses that could potentially damage the consumer's hard disk and its contents. Finally, if software is illegally copied at work, the customer puts him or herself and the company he or she works for at a legal risk by pirating a program protected by copyright laws.
When you acquire counterfeit software, you're supporting disreputable businesses, to the detriment of tax-paying, legitimate businesses that provide jobs and salaries to good citizens and expand the economy. There is a good chance the profits from counterfeit software are funding additional illegitimate businesses. Legitimate companies do not receive funds from the distribution of counterfeit software, and frequently this loss of revenue leads to layoffs for workers in all related industries, from manufacturers to resellers. So, as you can see, acquiring counterfeit software doesn't impact software manufacturers only; it impacts everyone.
